Steel and alloying elements.

Any steel is an alloy, most basic ones are called 'carbon steels' - they are iron Fe alloyed with carbon C. But to achieve improved mechanical properties other alloying elements often added in addition to the carbon. Alloy steel is steel that is alloyed with a variety of elements in total amounts between 1.0% and 50% by weight. The following is a range of improved properties in alloy steels (as compared to carbon steels): strength, hardness, toughness, wear resistance, corrosion resistance, hardenability, and hot hardness. To achieve some of these improved properties the metal may require heat treating.

Alloying elements (alloyants) include manganese (the most common one), nickel, chromium, molybdenum, vanadium, silicon, and boron. Less common alloyants include aluminium, cobalt, copper, cerium, niobium, titanium, tungsten, tin, zinc, lead, and zirconium. Elements like hydrogen H, nitrogen N, phosphorus P and sulfur S are rarely added deliberately, but they are very important nevertheless as they can drastically decrease certain mechanical properties. For example, sulfur leads to hot shortness and reduces the resistance to pitting corrosion.
